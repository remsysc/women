How Women’s Month Came to Be

Written by Sairah Mae R. Saipudin

The beginning of the celebration of Women’s Month can trace its roots in the socialist and labor movements in the United States of America. The first ever Women’s Day happened in New York City on 28 February, 1909 as a national observance organized by the Socialist Party. This was done to commemorate the one-year anniversary of the strikes by garment workers in New York, where a large number of women marched through lower Manhattan to Union Square to fight for economic rights.

International Women’s Day (IWD) was first celebrated on 19 March 1911. A million women and men rallied in support of women's rights. On 25 March 1911, the tragic fire at the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory in New York took the lives of more than 140 working women, exposing the dire labor conditions faced by women. This sparked numerous demonstrations organized by the National Women’s Trade Union League.

In Europe, the Socialist Women’s International Conference suggested that 8 March should be IWD to recognize all working women globally. By 1975, the United Nations officially designated 8 March as a global celebration of women’s contributions, courage, and achievements.

In the Philippines, former President Corazon C. Aquino signed Proclamation No. 224 in 1988, declaring the first week of March as Women’s Week and 8 March as Women’s Rights and International Peace Day. Later that year, she designated March as Women’s Role in History Month. These initiatives continue to honor women’s contributions and raise awareness for gender equality and women’s rights.
